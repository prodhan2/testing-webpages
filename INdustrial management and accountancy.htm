(A)Define business.

Business is the process of producing or selling goods or services for profit. It involves activities like making products, advertising them, and selling them to customers who want to buy them. Businesses can be small or large and can involve one person or many people working together. The goal of a business is to make money by providing products or services that people want or need.

(b) What are the important features of business?

The important features of a business are:

  1. Economic activity: A business is an economic activity that involves the production or exchange of goods and services for a profit.
  2. Profit motive: The primary objective of a business is to earn a profit by providing products or services that are valued by customers.
  3. Risk and uncertainty: Businesses operate in an environment of risk and uncertainty due to factors like competition, changing customer preferences, and fluctuations in the economy.
  4. Legal entity: A business is a legal entity that can own property, enter into contracts, and sue or be sued in court.
  5. Investment of capital: Businesses require an investment of capital in order to purchase equipment, hire employees, and produce goods or services.
  6. Continuity: A business is typically established with the aim of operating for an extended period of time and therefore needs to ensure its continuity.
  7. Production and marketing: A business involves the production of goods or services and their marketing to customers in order to generate revenue.
  8. Interdependence: Businesses are interdependent on various stakeholders including customers, suppliers, and employees, and must maintain positive relationships with them in order to operate successfully.

Overall, these features help to define the nature and scope of a business and distinguish it from other types of economic activities.

 

(c) Mention the different types of business entities.

There are several different types of business entities, each with their own advantages and disadvantages. The most common types of business entities include:

1.    Sole proprietorship: A business owned and operated by a single individual. The owner is personally liable for the debts and obligations of the business.

2.    Partnership: A business owned by two or more individuals who share the profits and losses. There are two types of partnerships: general partnerships and limited partnerships.

3.    Limited liability company (LLC): A hybrid entity that combines the flexibility and tax benefits of a partnership with the limited liability of a corporation.

4.    Corporation: A legal entity that is owned by shareholders and managed by a board of directors. Corporations provide limited liability protection to their owners.

5.    Cooperative: A business owned and operated by a group of individuals for their mutual benefit. Cooperatives can take many forms, such as agricultural, consumer, or worker cooperatives.

6.    Franchise: A business model in which a company (the franchisor) licenses its name, products, and services to an independent business owner (the franchisee).

7.    Nonprofit organization: A business entity that is organized for a charitable, educational, religious, or other socially beneficial purpose. Nonprofits are exempt from paying taxes and can accept tax-deductible donations.

Each type of business entity has its own advantages and disadvantages in terms of liability, taxation, management structure, and funding options. It's important for entrepreneurs to choose the type of business entity that best suits their goals and circumstances.

(b) Discuss the types of industries with examples

Industries can be classified into various types based on their nature, size, products, and services. Some of the common types of industries are:

  1. Primary industry: These are industries involved in the extraction and collection of natural resources. Examples of primary industries include mining, agriculture, fishing, forestry, and oil and gas extraction.
  2. Secondary industry: These are industries involved in the processing and manufacturing of raw materials into finished goods. Examples of secondary industries include steel production, automobile manufacturing, and textile production.
  3. Tertiary industry: These are industries involved in the provision of services to consumers and businesses. Examples of tertiary industries include banking, education, healthcare, hospitality, and transportation.
  4. Quaternary industry: These are industries involved in knowledge-based activities such as research and development, information technology, and consulting services.
  5. Quinary industry: These are industries that involve high-level decision-making and policy development. Examples of quinary industries include government, education, and research organizations.
  6. Heavy industry: These are industries that involve the production of large and heavy products such as machinery, construction materials, and transportation equipment.
  7. Light industry: These are industries that involve the production of small and light products such as clothing, food products, and consumer electronics.
  8. Sunrise industry: These are industries that are new and emerging, with the potential for rapid growth and development. Examples of sunrise industries include renewable energy, biotechnology, and e-commerce.
  9. Sunset industry: These are industries that are declining and facing challenges due to changing market trends, technology advancements, or regulatory changes. Examples of sunset industries include traditional printing, film photography, and some types of traditional retail.

Overall, industries can be classified in different ways, and businesses need to be aware of the trends and challenges in their respective industries in order to compete effectively and succeed over the long term.

 

3. (a) Name the general principles proposed by Fayol.

Henri Fayol was a French management theorist who proposed a set of fourteen general principles of management that could be applied to all types of organizations. These principles are often referred to as Fayol's "14 Principles of Management". Here is a list of the principles:

  1. Division of work: Work should be divided into small tasks to achieve efficiency and specialization.
  2. Authority and responsibility: Authority and responsibility should be clearly defined and balanced.
  3. Discipline: Employees should follow rules and procedures to maintain order and stability.
  4. Unity of command: Each employee should receive orders from only one supervisor to avoid confusion.
  5. Unity of direction: The organization should have a single, unified direction to achieve its objectives.
  6. Subordination of individual interests to the general interest: The interests of the organization should take precedence over individual interests.
  7. Remuneration: Employees should receive fair compensation for their work.
  8. Centralization: Decision-making authority should be centralized at the top of the organization.
  9. Scalar chain: There should be a clear hierarchy of authority and communication channels.
  10. Order: Materials and people should be in the right place at the right time.
  11. Equity: Employees should be treated with justice and equity.
  12. Stability of tenure: Employees should be given job security and not be subjected to frequent turnover.
  13. Initiative: Employees should be encouraged to take initiative and contribute to the organization's success.
  14. Esprit de corps: A spirit of unity and teamwork should be fostered among employees.

These principles are still widely used today as a basis for organizational management, and they continue to provide useful insights into the practices that can help organizations achieve success.

 

 

(b)                   Discuss Maslow's hierarchy of needs.

(c)    Abraham Maslow's hierarchy of needs is a theory in psychology that explains the different levels of human needs that motivate behavior. The theory proposes that individuals have five categories of needs, which are arranged in a hierarchical order. According to Maslow, once a lower level of need is satisfied, individuals move on to the next level in the hierarchy. The five categories of needs are:

(d)    Physiological needs: These are the basic needs required for survival, such as air, water, food, shelter, and clothing. They are the most fundamental needs and must be satisfied first before moving on to higher levels of needs.

(e)    Safety needs: Once physiological needs are satisfied, individuals seek safety and security. This includes the need for stability, order, and protection from danger, such as job security, a safe environment, and financial stability.

(f)     Love and belonging needs: Once safety needs are fulfilled, individuals seek social connection and a sense of belonging. This includes the need for love, affection, friendship, and family.

(g)    Esteem needs: Once love and belonging needs are fulfilled, individuals seek to gain self-esteem and the respect of others. This includes the need for achievement, recognition, status, and confidence.

(h)    Self-actualization needs: Once all other needs are satisfied, individuals seek to achieve their full potential and realize their own unique talents and abilities. This includes the need for personal growth, creativity, and self-fulfillment.

(i)      Maslow believed that self-actualization is the ultimate goal of human behavior and that individuals must satisfy lower-level needs before they can move on to higher-level needs. The theory suggests that motivation is driven by the desire to satisfy these needs, and that the hierarchy of needs can help explain why individuals behave in certain ways. The theory has been widely applied in fields such as management, marketing, and education, and has contributed to a better understanding of human behavior and motivation

4. (a) What is accounting?

Accounting is the process of identifying, measuring, recording, and communicating financial information about an organization to various stakeholders. The main goal of accounting is to provide accurate and relevant financial information that helps stakeholders make informed decisions.

Make a discussion on the elements of financial statements.

Financial statements are formal reports that provide information about an organization's financial performance and position over a specific period of time. The elements of financial statements are the fundamental components that make up the information presented in financial statements. The International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) and the Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) recognize the following elements of financial statements:

  1. Assets: Assets are resources that an organization owns and has control over. They can be tangible, such as cash, inventory, and property, plant, and equipment, or intangible, such as patents, trademarks, and goodwill. Assets are typically recorded on the balance sheet at their historical cost, which is the amount paid to acquire them.
  2. Liabilities: Liabilities are obligations that an organization owes to others. They can be current, such as accounts payable and short-term loans, or long-term, such as mortgages and bonds. Liabilities are recorded on the balance sheet at their current fair value, which is the amount that would be required to settle the obligation.
  3. Equity: Equity represents the residual interest in the assets of an organization after liabilities are deducted. It includes common stock, retained earnings, and other reserves. Equity is recorded on the balance sheet and is calculated as the difference between assets and liabilities.
  4. Revenues: Revenues are inflows of economic resources resulting from the sale of goods or services. They are recognized when earned, regardless of when payment is received. Revenue is recorded on the income statement as the top line, above expenses.
  5. Expenses: Expenses are outflows of economic resources incurred in the process of generating revenues. They are recognized when incurred, regardless of when payment is made. Expenses are recorded on the income statement below revenues, and include items such as cost of goods sold, operating expenses, and interest expenses.
  6. Gains and losses: Gains and losses are other items that affect an organization's financial position or performance but are not directly related to its primary operations. For example, gains or losses from the sale of a long-term asset or the settlement of a lawsuit. They are recorded on the income statement separately from revenues and expenses.

The information presented in the financial statements can be used by various stakeholders, including investors, creditors, management, and regulators, to assess an organization's financial performance and position. By understanding the elements of financial statements and how they are interrelated, stakeholders can make informed decisions about investing, lending, or managing an organization.

Accounting information is used by different people who have an interest in an organization's financial situation. Some of the main users of accounting information are:

  1. Business Owners and Managers: They use accounting information to make decisions about the organization's operations, investment opportunities, and financial stability.
  2. Investors: They use accounting information to decide whether to invest in a company and to evaluate the company's financial performance.

  4. Employees: They use accounting information to assess their job security and understand how their efforts contribute to the overall financial health of the company.
  5. Government Regulators: They use accounting information to enforce compliance with tax laws, environmental regulations, and other laws.
  6. Customers and Suppliers: They use accounting information to assess the financial stability of the company and to evaluate the risks and benefits of doing business with the organization.

Accounting information helps these users make informed decisions about an organization's operations, financial performance, and stability. By providing reliable financial information, accounting information helps ensure that organizations are accountable to their stakeholders and that they can make decisions that are in the best interests of their stakeholders.

(a) Define Business.

Business refers to any organization or activity engaged in the provision of goods or services to customers, in exchange for payment or profit. A business may operate for-profit or non-profit, with the primary objective of meeting the needs and wants of its customers while generating revenue and profits. A business typically involves the production, buying and selling, marketing, and distribution of goods or services, and may involve various stakeholders such as investors, employees, customers, and suppliers. Business activities may take place in various sectors including manufacturing, service, retail, and finance, and may vary in scale from small sole proprietorships to large multinational corporations.

(b) What do you mean by 'Extractive Industry"?

Extractive industries are the businesses that take raw materials, including oil, coal, gold, iron, copper and other minerals, from the earth. The industrial processes for extracting minerals include drilling and pumping, quarrying, and mining.

(c) How commerce can help to eliminate the barriers for conducting a business perfectly in Bangladesh?

Commerce can play a crucial role in eliminating barriers to conducting business in Bangladesh in several ways:

  1. Technology: The application of digital technology in commerce can help to reduce barriers to doing business in Bangladesh. For example, e-commerce platforms can provide access to new markets and customers, improve supply chain management, and reduce transaction costs.
  2. Trade agreements: Bangladesh can benefit from participation in international trade agreements that reduce barriers to trade, such as tariffs, quotas, and other trade barriers. These agreements can provide access to new markets, promote investment, and improve the competitiveness of domestic firms.
  3. Business education: Commerce can help to provide access to education and training for entrepreneurs, businesses, and other stakeholders. This education can focus on best practices in management, accounting, marketing, and other areas that are essential for business success.
  4. Infrastructure: Commerce can help to improve the infrastructure needed for conducting business, such as transportation, telecommunications, and energy systems. This infrastructure can facilitate access to markets, reduce the cost of doing business, and promote economic growth.
  5. Access to finance: Commerce can help to provide access to finance for businesses, through the development of financial institutions, such as banks, and other sources of funding, such as venture capital and private equity. Access to finance is critical for starting and growing a business, and can help to reduce barriers to entry for new businesses.

In summary, commerce can help to eliminate barriers to conducting business in Bangladesh through the application of technology, participation in trade agreements, business education, infrastructure development, and access to finance.

(a) What is sole proprietorship form of business?

Sole proprietorship is a form of business organization in which an individual owns and operates the business. In a sole proprietorship, the owner is personally liable for all of the business's debts and obligations, and the business's profits and losses are reported on the owner's personal income tax return.

Some key characteristics of a sole proprietorship include:

  1. Ownership: The business is owned by a single individual who makes all the decisions and takes all the risks.
  2. Liability: The owner is personally responsible for all of the business's debts and legal obligations. This means that the owner's personal assets are at risk in the event of a lawsuit or bankruptcy.
  3. Taxes: The business's income is reported on the owner's personal income tax return, and the owner is responsible for paying all applicable taxes.
  4. Control: The owner has complete control over the business and is responsible for all decisions.
  5. Flexibility: Sole proprietorships are relatively easy to set up and operate, with few legal requirements or formalities.

Some advantages of a sole proprietorship include:

  1. Simple and inexpensive to set up and operate.
  2. Complete control over the business and decision-making.
  3. No need to share profits or ownership with others.
  4. Flexibility to change or pivot the business as needed.

Some disadvantages of a sole proprietorship include:

  1. Unlimited liability, which means the owner's personal assets are at risk in the event of a lawsuit or bankruptcy.
  2. Difficulty in raising capital, as lenders may be hesitant to loan money to a sole proprietorship.
  3. Limited expertise and resources, as the owner must handle all aspects of the business.
  4. Limited life, as the business is tied to the owner's lifespan and may not continue after the owner's death or retirement.

(c) State the basic features of Joint Stock Company.

Basic features of Joint Stock Company:

  1. Legal entity: A joint stock company is a separate legal entity from its owners and can own property, enter into contracts, and sue or be sued in its own name.
  2. Limited liability: Shareholders of a joint stock company have limited liability for the company's debts and obligations. Their liability is limited to the amount of their investment in the company.
  3. Transferable shares: The ownership of a joint stock company is divided into transferable shares, which can be bought and sold by shareholders.
  4. Perpetual succession: A joint stock company has perpetual succession, meaning it can continue to exist even if its shareholders or directors change.
  5. Separate management: The management of a joint stock company is separate from its ownership. Shareholders elect a board of directors to manage the company, and the board hires a management team to carry out day-to-day operations.
  6. Large capital base: Joint stock companies can raise large amounts of capital by selling shares to the public.
  7. Regulatory compliance: Joint stock companies are subject to extensive regulation and reporting requirements, which can be costly and time-consuming.

(a) Define Management.

Management can be defined as the process of planning, organizing, directing, and controlling resources (people, finances, materials, and information) to achieve organizational goals and objectives in an effective and efficient manner. It involves making decisions, setting goals, and designing strategies to achieve those goals while also coordinating and overseeing the work of others to ensure that the organization's resources are being used in the most productive way possible. Effective management is essential for the success of any organization, as it helps to ensure that resources are being used efficiently and that the organization is moving towards its desired outcomes.

(b) Discuss in brief the functions of Management.

The functions of management can be described as follows:

  1. Planning: This involves setting goals and objectives for the organization, and creating a plan to achieve them. It involves analyzing the current situation, identifying opportunities and threats, and formulating strategies to reach desired outcomes.
  2. Organizing: This involves arranging resources and tasks to achieve the organization's goals. This includes structuring the organization, determining job roles and responsibilities, and allocating resources such as budget, time, and staff.
  3. Staffing: This involves hiring and training employees to carry out the organization's plan. It includes recruiting, selecting, and training employees to perform their roles and responsibilities effectively.
  4. Directing: This involves leading and motivating employees to perform their tasks effectively. It includes communicating expectations, providing guidance and support, and building a positive work culture.
  5. Controlling: This involves monitoring progress towards goals and making necessary adjustments. It includes measuring performance, analyzing results, and making decisions to keep the organization on track towards its goals.

 

(c) Discuss the role of a manager in an organization that suggested by Henry Mintzberg.

Henry Mintzberg, a well-known management theorist, identified ten roles that managers play in an organization. These roles are divided into three categories: interpersonal, informational, and decisional.

  1. Interpersonal roles: a. Figurehead: In this role, the manager represents the organization in a symbolic way, performing ceremonial duties and acting as a spokesperson for the organization. b. Leader: In this role, the manager motivates, directs, and guides employees to achieve the organization's goals. c. Liaison: In this role, the manager establishes and maintains relationships with people inside and outside the organization.
  2. Informational roles: a. Monitor: In this role, the manager collects and analyzes information about the organization's internal and external environment. b. Disseminator: In this role, the manager communicates information to others in the organization. c. Spokesperson: In this role, the manager communicates information to people outside the organization.
  3. Decisional roles: a. Entrepreneur: In this role, the manager initiates new projects and identifies new opportunities for the organization. b. Disturbance handler: In this role, the manager responds to unexpected situations and crises in the organization. c. Resource allocator: In this role, the manager decides how to allocate resources such as budget, staff, and equipment. d. Negotiator: In this role, the manager negotiates with others inside and outside the organization to reach agreements.

These roles show that a manager has a wide range of responsibilities and must be able to adapt to different situations in the organization. Effective managers must be able to balance these roles to meet the needs of the organization and achieve its goals.

(b) Star Company Limited produces a product selling at 30 per unit. Variable cost per unit is Tk. 18. Annual fixed cost is Tk. 1,50,000. Estimated sales for the period are 25,000 units. You are required to calculate:

(i) Break even point in units and taka.

(ii) The contribution margin and contribution margin ration.

(iii) The margin of safety.

(iv) The estimated profit for the period.

(v) The sales volume in units, if the company wants to earn profit of Tk. 1,50,000.

Given data: Selling price per unit = Tk. 30 Variable cost per unit = Tk. 18 Annual fixed cost = Tk. 1,50,000 Estimated sales for the period = 25,000 units

(i) Break-even point in units and taka: Contribution per unit = Selling price per unit - Variable cost per unit = Tk. 30 - Tk. 18 = Tk. 12 Break-even point (in units) = Fixed cost / Contribution per unit = Tk. 1,50,000 / Tk. 12 = 12,500 units Break-even point (in taka) = Break-even point (in units) x Selling price per unit = 12,500 x 30 = Tk. 3,75,000

(ii) The contribution margin and contribution margin ratio: Contribution margin = Selling price per unit - Variable cost per unit = Tk. 30 - Tk. 18 = Tk. 12 Contribution margin ratio = Contribution margin / Selling price per unit x 100 = 12 / 30 x 100 = 40%

(iii) The margin of safety: Margin of safety = Estimated sales - Break-even point = 25,000 - 12,500 = 12,500 units

(iv) The estimated profit for the period: Total revenue = Selling price per unit x Estimated sales = Tk. 30 x 25,000 = Tk. 7,50,000 Total variable cost = Variable cost per unit x Estimated sales = Tk. 18 x 25,000 = Tk. 4,50,000 Contribution margin = Total revenue - Total variable cost = Tk. 7,50,000 - Tk. 4,50,000 = Tk. 3,00,000 Total cost = Fixed cost + Total variable cost = Tk. 1,50,000 + Tk. 4,50,000 = Tk. 6,00,000 Profit = Contribution margin - Total cost = Tk. 3,00,000 - Tk. 6,00,000 = (Tk. 3,00,000)

(v) The sales volume in units, if the company wants to earn profit of Tk. 1,50,000: Profit = (Selling price per unit - Variable cost per unit) x Sales volume - Fixed cost Tk. 1,50,000 = (30 - 18) x Sales volume - Tk. 1,50,000 Sales volume = (Tk. 1,50,000 + Tk. 1,50,000) / 12 = 25,000 units

1. a) What is partnership?

A partnership is a business model where two or more people agree to run a business together and share the profits and losses. Each partner contributes to the business and shares in the management responsibilities.

b) What are the main features of a partnership business? Discuss.

The main features of a partnership business are:

  1. Agreement: A partnership is created through an agreement between two or more parties to carry on a business together.
  2. Shared Profits and Losses: The partners share the profits and losses of the business according to their agreed upon percentage of ownership.
  3. Unlimited Liability: Each partner is personally responsible for the debts and liabilities of the partnership.
  4. Joint Management: The partners share in the management of the business and make decisions together.
  5. Limited Life: A partnership typically has a limited lifespan, and may dissolve if a partner withdraws, dies or retires.

The accounting equation is a fundamental concept in accounting that represents the relationship between a company's assets, liabilities, and equity. The equation is:

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

This equation states that a company's assets must equal its liabilities plus its equity. Assets are the resources owned by the company that have economic value and can be used to generate future economic benefits. Liabilities are the company's obligations to pay for goods, services, or other obligations that have not yet been fulfilled. Equity represents the residual interest in the assets of the company after liabilities are deducted.

The accounting equation is the foundation of the balance sheet, which is a financial statement that provides a snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. By ensuring that the accounting equation is always in balance, accountants can accurately track a company's financial transactions and provide meaningful financial statements for decision-making purposes.

b)What do you mean by accounting cycle?

The accounting cycle is the process of accepting, recording, sorting, and crediting payments made and received within a business during a particular accounting period.

OR, The accounting cycle is a series of steps that businesses use to record, classify, and summarize their financial transactions over a particular accounting period, usually a month, quarter, or year. The accounting cycle is essential for producing accurate financial statements and ensuring that a company's financial records are in compliance with accounting principles and regulations.

The steps in the accounting cycle typically include:

  1. Analyzing and recording transactions: All financial transactions are analyzed and recorded in the company's journal in chronological order.
  2. Posting to the general ledger: Transactions are then posted to the appropriate accounts in the general ledger.
  3. Preparing an unadjusted trial balance: An unadjusted trial balance is prepared to ensure that the total debits and credits in the general ledger are equal.
  4. Making adjusting entries: Adjusting entries are made to update the general ledger accounts and ensure that they reflect the correct balances at the end of the accounting period.
  5. Preparing an adjusted trial balance: An adjusted trial balance is prepared to ensure that all the accounts are up-to-date and that the total debits and credits in the general ledger are equal.
  6. Preparing financial statements: The financial statements, including the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement, are prepared from the adjusted trial balance.
  7. Closing the books: The temporary accounts, such as revenue and expenses, are closed and transferred to the retained earnings account in the balance sheet.
  8. Preparing a post-closing trial balance: A post-closing trial balance is prepared to ensure that all the temporary accounts have been closed properly and that the total debits and credits in the general ledger are equal.

By completing these steps, businesses can ensure that their financial records are accurate, up-to-date, and compliant with accounting principles and regulations. The accounting cycle is essential for producing meaningful financial statements and providing the information needed to make informed business decisions

b) Ideal Company Limited produces a product selling at Tk. 50 per unit. Variable cost per unit is Tk.20. Annual fixed cost is Tk. 1,60,000. Estimated sales for the period are 35,000 units.

Required:

i. The break-even point in units and taka.

ii. The contribution margin and contribution margin ratio.

iii. The margin of safety and margin of safety ratio.

iv. The estimated profit for the period.

v. The sales volume in units if the company earns a profit of Tk. 1,80,000.

 

Given: Selling price per unit = Tk. 50 Variable cost per unit = Tk. 20 Annual fixed cost = Tk. 1,60,000 Estimated sales volume = 35,000 units

i. The break-even point in units and taka: Contribution per unit = Selling price per unit - Variable cost per unit Contribution per unit = Tk. 50 - Tk. 20 = Tk. 30

Break-even point (in units) = Fixed cost / Contribution per unit Break-even point (in units) = Tk. 1,60,000 / Tk. 30 = 5,333.33 units

Break-even point (in taka) = Break-even point (in units) * Selling price per unit Break-even point (in taka) = 5,333.33 * Tk. 50 = Tk. 2,66,666.67

ii. The contribution margin and contribution margin ratio: Contribution margin = Selling price per unit - Variable cost per unit Contribution margin = Tk. 50 - Tk. 20 = Tk. 30

Contribution margin ratio = Contribution per unit / Selling price per unit * 100% Contribution margin ratio = Tk. 30 / Tk. 50 * 100% = 60%

iii. The margin of safety and margin of safety ratio: Margin of safety (in units) = Actual sales - Break-even point (in units) Margin of safety (in units) = 35,000 - 5,333.33 = 29,666.67 units

Margin of safety ratio = Margin of safety (in units) / Actual sales * 100% Margin of safety ratio = 29,666.67 / 35,000 * 100% = 84.76%

iv. The estimated profit for the period: Profit = (Contribution per unit * Sales volume) - Fixed cost Profit = (Tk. 30 * 35,000) - Tk. 1,60,000 Profit = Tk. 5,50,000 - Tk. 1,60,000 Profit = Tk. 3,90,000

v. The sales volume in units if the company earns a profit of Tk. 1,80,000: Required sales volume = (Fixed cost + Profit) / Contribution per unit Required sales volume = (Tk. 1,60,000 + Tk. 1,80,000) / Tk. 30 Required sales volume = 11,333.33 units.
